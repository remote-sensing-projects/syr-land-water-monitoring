Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)

 

Methodology

Source data: Sentinel-2 MSI: MultiSpectral Instrument, Level-1C

Script: NDVI Composites

  1. Cloud masking. Each image in the Sentinel-2 time-series was cloud and cloud shadow masked using the Sen2CloudLess algorithm. Processing of Sentinel-2 Top of Atmosphere (TOA) imagery through sensor-invariant atmospheric correction (SIAC).*
  2. Mosaicking/compositing at monthly intervals. This process returned one image per month, with each pixel representing the mean value of all non-masked pixels available within the given month.
  3. Masking out areas that do not represent agricultural areas. To make NDVI mosaics of different agricultural seasons comparable, the same mask was applied to each NDVI mosaic. The mask was derived from a combination of annual cropland maps for the years 2017 to 2021 (see below for detailed description of the dataset).

Note: As rain-fed cropland extent changes throughout the years, (e.g. due to land abandonment during dry years or reactivation of land during wet years) NDVI mosaics can during dry years potentially include abandoned or fallow land which could lead to lower NDVI.

The cloud free coverage of the mosaics was 85-99%, with the vast majority reaching >95% coverage.

 

*Note: Due to the lack of Bottom of Atmosphere (BOA) imagery (Level-2A), i.e., atmospherically corrected imagery, for January 2017 to October 2018 in some parts of the region, TOA imagery (Level-1C) was used. Despite improvement of the NDVI accuracy through applying SIAC to TOA imagery, NDVI values tend to be slightly underestimated when compared to BOA imagery derived NDVI.

 

Interpretation

NDVI is used to quantify vegetation greenness and useful to understand vegetation health and vegetation density. High NDVI values correspond to healthy vegetation while low NDVI corresponds to less or no vegetation.

As for annual crops, high NDVI values (approx. 0.6 to 0.9) correspond to crops at their peak growth stage. Lower NDVI values reflect crops in their growing period before they have reached their peak growth stage (approx. 0.2-0.6) or bare soil (< 0.2) after harvest or during land preparation process before plant growth starts. Please note that NDVI values cannot directly be interpreted as crop yield.

 

 

Cropland Area

 

Methodology

Source data: Sentinel-2, Sentinel-1, Landsat-8, and MODIS Aqua and Terra, USRG SRTM (DEM)

Script (Training): Generating Training Data

Script (Classification): Classifying

 

The cropland maps were produced by UNOSAT through supervised image classification using training data and the Random Forest (RF) classifier. Processing was performed in Google Earth Engine (GEE) while the validation was mainly performed in ArcGIS Pro.

1.     Generating training data. Training samples were manually collected for nine land cover classes (annual crops, perennial crops, forest, fallow, grassland, rangeland, bare land, water, artificial) by visual interpretation of satellite imagery for five agricultural seasons, 2016/17 to 2020/21.

2.     Preparing a layer stack. The layer-stack included the following:

o   Cloud masked Sentinel-2/Landsat-8/MODIS monthly composites (bands: NDWI, NDVI, blue, green, red, nir, swir1, swir2). The Sentinel-2 time-series was snow masking using a combination of automatic and spectral based thresholds and manual delineation.

o   Sentinel-1 monthly composites (descending and ascending, bands: VV, VH).

o   Sentinel-1 monthly texture: entropy and inertia

o   Seasonal metrics: NDVI max, NDVI min, NDVI amp, NDVI stdev, VV max, VV min, VV amp, VV stdev

o   Slope

 

3.     Training the model. The RF was trained on the training points generated in Step 1, with associated features (pixel values extracted from the layer stack) from the corresponding month. I.e., training points from a given month were associate with satellite observations from the same month. Subsequently, all features extracted from one year were combined into one training dataset. Thereby, the RF was trained on data from one full calendar year.

4.     Classifying. The cropland maps were classified in annual batches, with the RF model trained on data from the corresponding year.

5.     Iteration. The training points were augmented based on the classification output, and the classifications rerun to improve classification results.

6.     Validation. The validation process was conducted in ArcGIS Pro. First, the 9-class land cover maps were reclassified into binary maps (cropland = 1, non-cropland = 0). To minimize the effects of spatial autocorrelation, validation points were sampled outside a minimum buffer of 1km of the training points. Stratified random sampling of validation points was generated for the binary classes. In the absence of in-situ measurements, the ground truthing process had to be done through satellite imagery. Using a GEE script, each point was compared against base satellite imagery to validate whether the classified label was correct or not.

 

The overall accuracy of the agricultural maps for each agricultural season ranged between 87% and 93% (see table below). The lowest accuracy was for the 2018 2019 agricultural season and the highest for the 2016 2017 season. Consistent with the overall accuracy metrics, the final kappa index values ranged between 0.72 to 0.85.

Perennial agriculture was often spectrally confused with forest, particularly forest with dense canopy cover. These two classes have similar spectral signatures, seasonal variability, and are both present along sloped terrain. This misclassification was especially prevalent along the hilly areas of Tartous and Lattakia governorates. Therefore, the woody vegetation class of the Annual Land Cover dataset (30m) by Dr. He Yin (to be published), was used to mask out pixels that were inaccurately classified as cropland when they represented forest.

Please note that due to the very wet season 2019/2020, NDVI of rain-fed cropland and other deciduous vegetation (e.g., grazing land) was significantly higher in this season. As NDVI tends to have a high feature importance for the applied image classifier (Random Forest), very high NDVI during 2019/2020 might have led to an overestimation of cropland area in this season.

 

Interpretation

 

Change maps

A change map compares two consecutive seasons, for example season 2019/20 and season 2020/2021, and displays where cropland remained stable (no change), was reactivated (gained), or lost (abandoned). In the app, one year refers to a season, i.e., 2021 refers October 2020 to September 2021 (inclusive).

There is a risk that cropland area in 2019/2020 was overestimated (see explanation above) which could have led to an overestimation of lost cropland area in the following season, 2020/2021. Lost areas therefore not necessarily indicate abandoned land but potentially include fields that experienced a significant decrease in crop productivity while they were still under management.

Note that inconsistencies in the spatial coverage of the land cover product used to mask forested areas led to inaccurately classified crop loss between the seasons 2019/2020 and 2020/2021 in sub-districts, Kasab, Masaada, Khan Arnaba and Rabeea.

 

 

 

 

Surface Water Area

 

Methodology

Source data: Sentinel-2, Sentinel-1, USRG SRTM (DEM), Merit Hydro (HAND) and GLAD Surface Water.

Script (Training): Generating Training Data

Script (Classification): Classifying

 

The Surface Water maps were generated through supervised image classification using training data and the Random Forest (RF) classifier. All processing was performed in Google Earth Engine (GEE).

  1. Generating training data. Monthly surface water datasets at 30 m resolution were available from GLAD 1999-2020. The GLAD surface water maps were used as sampling areas after applying a negative buffer of 30 m. 200 water and 800 land points were randomly sampled from each monthly map.
  2. Preparing a layer stack. The layer-stack included the following:

o   Cloud masked Sentinel-2 composites (bands: MNDWI, NDVI, blue, green, red, nir, swir1, swir2). The Sentinel-2 time-series was snow masking using a combination of automatic and spectral based thresholds and manual delineation.

o   Sentinel-1 monthly composites (descending and ascending, bands: VV, VH).

o   Elevation

o   Slope

o   HAND (Height Above Nearest Drainage)

  1. Training the model. The RF was trained on the training points generated in Step 1, with associated features (pixel values extracted from the layer stack) from the corresponding month. I.e. training points from a given month were associate with satellite observations from the same month. Subsequently, all features extracted from one year were combined into one training dataset. Thereby, the RF was trained on data from one full calendar year.
  2. Classifying. The monthly surface water maps were classified in annual batches, with the RF model trained on data from the corresponding year. The RF model for the 2021 classification was trained on 2020 data (no GLAD data was available for 2021).
  3. Iteration. The training was augmented based on the classification output, and the classifications rerun until a satisfactory result was achieved.
  4. Validation. The surface water classifications were validated visually. Attempts were made to calculate accuracies based on a subset of the training data. However, biases induced through the training data sampling technique resulted in unreliably high accuracies.

 

Interpretation

The surface water data are indicated in m. Temporal trends of surface water area should not be directly translated into trends of water level or water volume. Please also note that outliers in the time series (e.g., large drops in surface water area between two months) can be caused by errors in the classification, for example due to omission errors in cloud masking algorithm.

Standard Precipitation Index (SPI)

 

Methodology

Source data: Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation With Station Data, Version 2.0 Final (CHIRPS)

Script: SPI Composites

 

The SPI is a drought indicator developed by McKee et al. (1993) and describes the probability of variation from the normal precipitation over multiple years of data, on a monthly (or multiple months) time step.

The SPI is a z-score deviation from the mean of units of the standard deviation for a given accumulation period (e.g., 1, 3, 6, 12, 24 months). The mean and standard deviation used to calculate the SPI, are computed based for the same accumulation period within the preceding time period. In this script, total precipitation for any accumulation period is calculating by summing daily CHIRPS data.

As precipitation is usually not normally distributed, a gamma probability function is commonly used to calculate SPI, however this is not supported by the Earth Engine script. The resulting SPI can therefore just be used as an estimator.

 

The CHIRPS-based SPI composites have a spatial resolution of 0.05 degrees (≈ 5.5km).

 

Interpretation

This tool contains SPI of an accumulation period of 1 month (monthly SPI). Positive SPI values indicate greater than long-term mean precipitation (wet conditions) while negative values indicate less than long-term mean precipitation (dry conditions). Note that different months should not be compared (e.g., March 2021 and April 2021), however, the same months of different years are comparable (e.g., April 2020 and April 2021).
